2020 United States Presidential Election Statistics

The 2020 U.S. Presidential Election, held on November 3rd, stood out as one of the most contentious and closely-watched in recent history. Incumbent President Donald J. Trump, of the Republican Party, vied for a second term against the Democratic nominee, former Vice President Joseph R. Biden Jr. The backdrop of this election was unique, characterized by the global COVID-19 pandemic, which not only became a pivotal campaign issue but also significantly influenced the voting process. An unprecedented number of citizens chose to vote by mail, prompting discussions about postal system readiness and vote counting durations.

Election Results

The 2020 U.S. Presidential Election was a significant event, reflecting the nation's collective decision in selecting its leader for the next four years. With a total of 538 electoral votes available, a candidate needed at least 270 to secure the presidency. Joseph R. Biden Jr. of the Democratic Party won the election with 306 electoral votes, while incumbent President Donald J. Trump of the Republican Party secured 232 votes.

Understanding the Bubble Chart

The bubble chart provides a detailed visualization of voting margins across different states. By analyzing the size and color of each bubble, you can gauge the voting preference of each state at a glance. Notably, the chart highlights swing states, which do not have a consistent pattern of voting for one particular party. These states played a crucial role in determining the election outcome. Additionally, the chart showcases states that have historically been strongholds for the Democratic and Republican parties.

Candidate Approval Ratings Before the Election

Approval ratings offer insight into the public's perception of a candidate's performance and likability. Leading up to the election, both candidates experienced fluctuations in their ratings due to various events. President Trump's declaration of a state of emergency for the COVID-19 pandemic and his subsequent positive test for the virus negatively impacted his approval ratings. In contrast, the tragic death of George Floyd and the ensuing calls for racial justice positively influenced Biden's ratings as he was seen as a proponent for change and unity.

House of Representatives Post-Election

Following the election, the Democrats secured a majority in the House of Representatives with 222 seats, while the Republicans held 213 seats. This majority allows the Democrats significant power in legislative decision-making, enabling them to push forward their party's agenda and policies with greater ease.

Senate Dynamics

The U.S. Senate, post-election, comprised 48 Democrats, 50 Republicans, and 2 Independents. Though the Republicans had a numerical advantage, the two Independent senators caucused with the Democrats. Furthermore, with the Vice President's tie-breaking vote, the Democrats effectively held a slim majority of 51 to 50, allowing them to drive legislative decisions, confirm presidential appointments, and more.

Joe Biden's Approval Rating After Taking Office

Since his inauguration, President Joe Biden's approval ratings have seen some fluctuations. Events such as the Afghanistan withdrawal, the crisis at the U.S.-Mexico border, and rising inflation have led to a decline in his approval numbers. These events and their repercussions have been pivotal in shaping public opinion about the Biden administration's competence and decision-making.

Republican Candidates for the Next Election

As the next election cycle begins, several Republican candidates have emerged as potential nominees. Notable names include Donald Trump, Ron DeSantis, Nikki Haley, RamaSwamy, Mike Pence, and Robert F. Kennedy. Of these, Donald Trump remains a favorite, with DeSantis following closely behind. Their respective approval ratings, policies, and public personas will play a significant role in the upcoming primaries and the general election.
